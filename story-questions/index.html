// Story extraction — Jun 8 2026

Quick frame for what this is and why it matters before you dive in.

Most marketing in any category sounds the same. Same beats, same language, same claims, same "everybody knows" being repeated back to whoever's listening. That's why most ads don't work — they're saying things the reader has already heard a hundred times.

The ads and funnels that do work are almost always saying something nobody else in the space is saying. And the reason they can say it is because there's a real story or experience underneath it, with specifics that are interesting, contradicting, and resonating all at the same time. Interesting because the detail's unusual. Contradicting because it argues with what people already believe. Resonating because it's true and the reader can feel that.

Most brands can't pull that off because they're inventing the contradiction from the outside. You don't have that problem — you have a real one. PANDAS, the forty medications, the $50K, what your family went through, what you watch for in your daughters now. None of that is theory. It happened. And the specifics from inside it are the unfair advantage nobody in this category can copy.

// The picture I keep in my head

Think of all the marketing in your space as a crowd at a beach. Everybody's wearing the same color. The same "natural this," the same "doctor-formulated that," the same "support your child's nervous system." Nobody stands out, so nothing gets seen.

To be the one a desperate parent actually spots from across that crowd, you have to be standing somewhere nobody else is standing. Not louder — somewhere different. And the only way to know where that somewhere is is to know the crowd intimately enough to see the gap.

Your real story is the gap. Every specific detail you give me is another step toward that gap — the place where what you say is interesting, contradicts what they've been told, and rings true at the same time. That's the spot where ads stop bouncing off and start landing.

So that's why I'm asking. The whole year of marketing we build for the brand traces back to the answers you give in this exercise. Funnels, ads, emails, the founder story on the PDP, video scripts, advertorials — every one of those will pull lines, scenes, sentences, and specifics from what you record here.

Every concrete detail is a future ad. The more concrete you get, the more we have to work with.

// How to do this so it doesn't feel like work

Don't type these. Open the voice memo app, put your AirPods in, and literally go for a walk.

A walk is the work. Not a break from the work, not a thing you do after the work — the walk is the session. Something about being outside, moving, and not staring at a screen is what lets the answers actually surface. Most of my best work happens this way.

Read a question, walk for a few minutes while you talk through your answer, then read the next one. Don't pre-plan. The first thing that comes out is usually the realest.

An hour is a good target. If you want to spread it across a couple of walks over the week, that works too. If you find yourself thinking of something three days later, open the memo and add it — coming back to the same question almost always surfaces something you didn't have the first time.

For questions tagged pull in family, the best answers come from calling whoever was driving this with you and letting them remember out loud. For the ones tagged as a dad now, those are the ones only you can answer from where you are now.

The small stuff is the gold. The details you'd brush past as "not important" are usually the ones a parent reading would recognize from her own life. So lean toward specifics, not summaries.

You don't have to go in order. Start wherever pulls you. Skip anything that doesn't.

// A quick story on why I'm pushing for specifics

The most famous direct-response ad in golf history is John Carlton's "Amazing Secret Discovered By One-Legged Golfer Adds 50 Yards To Your Drives." Ran for years. Made millions.

Carlton didn't invent the angle. He got it by interviewing the golf instructor whose product he was writing for. They sat together for almost an hour and Carlton got nothing usable — until the instructor mentioned, as a throwaway, the one-legged guy he'd watched at a driving range whose swing had quietly inspired his whole technique. To him, that detail was old news. He almost didn't bring it up because he didn't think it was important.

That's the pattern. The throwaway is usually the gold. So when something feels too small or random to be worth saying — say it anyway.

01

The Things That Piss You Off

The beliefs out there that you know are wrong because you lived the counter-evidence.

  1. 01What's the medical or parenting "everybody knows" about kids with tics or PANDAS that you think is most flat-out wrong? The thing that makes you go no, actually — at a dinner table, in a Facebook group, from a doctor.
  2. 02Looking back at the 40 medications — was there a point where you realized the medications themselves were part of the problem, not just failing to fix it? When did that flip happen?
  3. 03When the doctors kept pointing at "anxiety" or "Tourette's" or "behavioral" as the cause — what was actually causing it? Walk me through how you'd explain it to another parent now.
  4. 04What's the position in this whole conversation that nobody is currently taking that you wish someone would? Not the contrarian take that's been said a million times — the one you'd quietly say to a friend over coffee that you don't see anyone actually defending publicly.
  5. 05If you had to put up a poster of just one sentence — about what you went through, what it taught you, or what you'd want the world to know about kids' health — what's the first sentence that comes out? Don't overthink. First draft. Whatever lands.
02

What's Going On In Her Head

The parent reader — what she's thinking and seeing and hearing.

  1. 06A mom of a kid with sudden-onset tics is at her kitchen table at 11pm scrolling her phone. What's running through her head right now? Not what she'd say to a doctor — what she's thinking when nobody's listening.
  2. 07When other parents have come to your family asking about a kid with similar symptoms — what do they say first? What's the question they always lead with, and what's the question they're actually asking underneath? Pull in family
  3. 08Where are parents in this situation getting their information right now? Who are they listening to, what's in their info diet — the Facebook groups, the doctors, the books, the influencers, the subreddits?
  4. 09What's a sentence somebody said during your whole experience that you still remember word for word? Doesn't have to be the most important one. Just the one that stuck. Pull in family
  5. 10Walk me into one specific doctor's office or hospital room from this whole period. Just one. Where was it, what did the room look like, who was there, what was being said? Don't generalize. Render the one scene that comes up first when you think about it.
03

The Parts You Don't Usually Talk About

The emotional reality underneath. The texture nobody outside the family knows.

  1. 11The funnel mentions you started smoking to numb yourself even though you knew it wasn't a real solution. Walk me through that decision — when did it start, what was happening that made it feel like the only thing left? This one's heavy. Skip if you'd rather.
  2. 12What was the moment you took control — that "I was done outsourcing my health to treatments that failed" turn? What were you doing, what triggered it, what did you decide?
  3. 13When you were at your worst, was the real you still in there? What was it like trying to be you while your body and mind were running their own program?
  4. 14Did anyone ever suggest your symptoms were behavioral — that you were doing it on purpose, looking for attention, "acting out"? What did that land like on you and on your parents at the time?
  5. 15What did the tics actually look like in your body? Was it your shoulder, your face, your throat, your eyes — was it the same one over and over or did it move around? Was it visible to other people or could you sometimes mask it? The specific physical picture is what a parent reading would recognize in her own kid.
  6. 16What did a tic feel like from the inside, in the seconds before, during, and after? Was there a buildup, a release, a relief, a shame? Nobody outside the body knows this. You're one of the few people who could ever write it down.
  7. 17What did you have to give up during the worst stretch that other kids your age were doing? Sports, friendships, schoolwork, parties, dating, normal teenage stuff. The specific things you lost or stepped away from.
  8. 18Was there ever a stretch where you felt like you'd lost yourself — like you didn't recognize who you were anymore? And was there a moment, even briefly, when you came back? What brought you back?
04

As The Dad You Are Now

The vantage point only you have. Nobody else in this category can answer these.

  1. 19If one of your daughters started showing what you showed at the age you were — what would you do first? What would you not do? What would you tell yourself, knowing what you know now? As a dad now
  2. 20What do you refuse to let into your daughters' lives because of what you went through? Foods, medications, products, doctors, environments — the list of "absolutely not in this house" and why each one's on it. As a dad now
  3. 21What do you watch for in your daughters — quietly, that they don't know about — because of what you know now? What are the early signs you'd catch immediately that another parent would miss? As a dad now
  4. 22Have you ever had a moment with one of your daughters that hit you because it would have been impossible for you at the same age, given what you were going through? A small, ordinary moment that meant something extra. As a dad now

The Receipts

The real numbers and names that make the story unforgeable.

  2. 24Rough count — how many doctors and specialists across the whole journey? How many neurologists, how many other specialties? Pull in family

  4. 26Are there any documents from this period that still exist? Prescription bottles, EOBs, doctor's notes, photos, home videos, journal entries. Even knowing what's still around is helpful. Pull in family
06

The Weird Specifics

The Carlton one-legged-golfer stuff. The details you'd never think to mention.

  1. 27What's the weirdest, most specific moment you remember from the worst of it? Something with nothing to do with the medical part — what was on TV, what was for dinner, what your house smelled like, what song was playing, what someone was wearing. Pick the one scene that pops into your head first and render it. The kind of detail that feels random is usually the gold.
  2. 28Is there a single image from that period — actual or in your head — that, if a parent saw it on her phone screen, would make her stop scrolling and feel something?
07

What Changed, And What's True Now

The proof that this isn't denial wearing a hopeful costume.

  1. 29When you started addressing the inflammation directly — what was the first thing that actually changed? Slow or fast? Was there a moment of wait, is this real before you let yourself believe it?
  2. 30What does an ordinary Tuesday look like for you now? Where you live, what you do, the small ordinary things — including being a dad to your three daughters — that during the worst times wouldn't have been possible.
  3. 31Who or what was the source that first pointed you in the direction that ended up working? A friend's mom, a Reddit comment, a podcast, a book, a single conversation? Sometimes there's one person or one moment that changed the trajectory. Who was it for you?
  4. 32What's something you did or felt for the first time after recovery — that surprised you? A small moment of "wait, I haven't been able to do this in years" or "I forgot what this felt like." The first-time-after moments.
  5. 33If a teenager right now is sitting in his room having the same kind of day you used to have — what's the one thing you'd want him to know? Not the parent reading this. Him.
08

Why You Built This

Not the marketing answer. The real one.

  1. 34If you had to explain to your daughters in twenty years why their dad built Green Valley Nutrition — what would you tell them?
  2. 35What do you believe now about kids' health, the medical system, or how parents should approach this stuff that you didn't believe before PANDAS? What's the worldview shift that came out of all of it?
  3. 36What would have been different about your childhood if your family had had access to what you sell now, back then? What would the trajectory have looked like?
