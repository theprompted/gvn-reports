The fastest way to scale

GVN has spent the last 30+ days running paid acquisition across multiple customer-acquisition funnels in parallel. That spend has already produced data — which audiences are converting, which pages are closing, which funnels are gaining traction. This report answers a single question: given everything the paid traffic has already taught us, what's the fastest, lowest-risk path to scale from here?

Methodology

The premise: GVN has already paid for the experiment. Every dollar of the last 30 days has produced signal — what converts, what doesn't, which audiences Meta's algorithm keeps serving, which it deprioritizes, which funnels have the LP architecture to close, which don't. The fastest scaling path is the one where the signal is already there and the infrastructure is already built — not the one that requires a new bet.

To find that path, the analysis worked from the landing pages backward — because the LP is where the customer actually converts, and the page's language, objection-handling, and offer structure reveal exactly who the brand believes the buyer is, plus how complete the funnel actually is.

Every active landing page in the GVN ad account was rendered live in a browser (so JavaScript-loaded content was captured), scrolled top-to-bottom, and the full visible text extracted. Each page was then read in full. The avatar inference comes from what the page itself reveals: who the headline addresses, what fears get disarmed early, what mechanism the page uses to build credibility, who shows up in the testimonials, and what the offer structure (price tiers, guarantee length, objection-handling sequence) signals about the buyer's likely caution level.

Spend, ad-count, purchase, and ROAS data pulled live from the Meta Marketing API across both GVN ad accounts (act_1401319770002379 main + act_172619599133100 personal). Window: Apr 14 – May 13, 2026. "Active" means the ad, ad set, and campaign are all currently status ACTIVE. 211 ads classified across 13 destination URLs.

Synthesis — the fastest path to scale

The right question isn't "which avatar should we build a funnel for?" — that's the slow, expensive path. The right question is: "given that we've already paid to run 9 funnels for the last 30+ days, which one is the data telling us is closest to ready, and what's the lowest-risk way to scale it?"

What the paid data already tells us

The account has been running an experiment for the last 30+ days — paid budget across 9 distinct cold-acquisition avatars, plus retargeting. Each funnel has its own LP, mechanism story, and offer structure, and each has produced its own signal. The advertorials and offer pages already exist as real assets that took meaningful time and creative effort to build. The question now is which of those assets has produced the clearest "ready to scale" signal — where the audience is converting, the LP is closing, and the next dollar of investment compounds rather than starts from zero.

Why concentration is the unlock

The deeper the quiver of arrows aimed at one avatar, the faster that avatar scales. Each ad is an arrow. The more distinct angles, hooks, and emotional entry points you have aimed at the same person, the more chances you have to land the conversion. That's the lever — not the LP, not the offer, not the audience. The creative inventory itself.

Concentrating effort on one avatar is how the quiver gets deep enough to compound. The path to scale is more ads for the same person.

The fastest path to scale: PANDAS

The data already paid for points at PANDAS as the lowest-risk lever — not because it's a hopeful bet, but because the experiment has already run and produced a winning configuration on every dimension that matters for scale-up:

  • The audience is already validated by paid traffic. 1.36x ROAS on $1,984 over 30 days — the only cold-acquisition funnel above first-order break-even at meaningful spend. Meta has identified the audience, and they're converting. Scaling is putting more behind a result that's already happening.
  • The funnel infrastructure is already built. Two-page architecture (advertorial → offer), strong objection-handling on both, 365-day guarantee. The next dollar of investment goes into amplifying a working funnel, not building one. Compare to Kid Sleep, Autism Sleep Crisis, or Weight Loss — sharp angles, no proven offer page, no proven LP architecture. To scale those, you'd be building from a much earlier stage.
  • The credibility shell is already paid for. "NEURODIVERGENCE INSTITUTE" white-labeling is calibrated to a parent who distrusts supplement marketing. Building that brand-laundering layer from scratch for any other avatar is weeks of work. PANDAS has it.
  • The competitive moat is unreplicable. Ethan had PANDAS as a kid. The $50K-spent-on-failed-therapies story is documented, not invented. Spending more here deepens an advantage no competitor in kids-CBD can match. Spending more on Depleted or Weight Loss puts GVN in a category with hundreds of competitors making identical mechanism claims.
  • The audience compounds organically. PANDAS Network groups, PANS/PANDAS specialist communities, parent Facebook groups — small but tight-knit. A buyer here recommends the product to other PANDAS moms in a way an "anxious adult" buyer doesn't recommend it to other anxious adults. Word-of-mouth ROI compounds in ways the larger "Depleted" or "Weight Loss" audiences won't.

The other funnels are real assets that took real work to build, and they'll be there to return to. But none of them currently shows the same alignment of signals PANDAS does, and concentrating effort on one funnel where the experiment has already produced a winning configuration is the lowest-risk, fastest path to scale. The experiment has been paid for. PANDAS is what the data points at.

Is the market big enough to scale?

The natural objection to concentrating on PANDAS: isn't that audience too small? The answer depends on how the funnel is read — and the math comes out comfortable once you look at the actual numbers.

The funnel isn't strict-PANDAS-only

The advertorial does not require a confirmed PANDAS diagnosis to convert. It speaks to "my child changed suddenly," "tics appeared overnight," "doctors dismissed me," "I've tried everything." Strict PANDAS itself is genuinely rare — peer-reviewed US incidence is ~6,200 new diagnoses per year, and NIMH calls overall prevalence "rare and unknown." But Ethan's story and the funnel's framing speak to a much broader pool: parents of kids with tics, sudden behavioral changes, childhood OCD, and pediatrician-dismissed neuropsychiatric symptoms.

The addressable parent pool

US parents whose kid has the problem the funnel speaks to:

  • Chronic tic disorders & Tourette syndrome: ~730K–2.2M US children (1–3% of children). CDC · Knight 2012 meta-analysis
  • Childhood OCD: ~365K–1.7M US children (0.5–2.3%). NIMH
  • Provisional / transient tics in school-age children: 5–20% will experience tics at some point — millions of kids whose parents have seen the "sudden onset" moment Ethan describes.

De-duplicated for the overlap between OCD and tic disorders (~30% comorbidity), the parent pool comes out to:

  • Conservative read: ~1.5 million US parents — counting only chronic tic disorders + low-end OCD.
  • Central read: ~5 million US parents — central prevalence estimates plus half of school-age kids currently experiencing provisional tics.

What it takes to hit 50 orders/day

50 orders/day × 365 days = ~18,250 orders per year. Assuming a normal mix of one-time and Subscribe-and-Save repeat purchases (35–50% repeat for a daily-use kids supplement), new customers needed are between 9,000 and 18,000 per year.

The conversion rate required to hit 50 orders/day sits between 0.2% and 1.2% of the addressable parent pool over a full year. Even at the most conservative read — smallest plausible market, zero repeat customers — it's 1.2%. At the central read with normal repeat behavior, it's 0.2–0.3%. For a well-targeted DTC funnel converting on a real, painful problem, capturing that small a sliver of an audience over a year is comfortably within reach.

The actual AOV from BOGOKIDS-coded orders pulled from Shopify during the 30-day audit was $80.15 (18 orders / $1,442.67) — most orders are the single BOGO at $69.99 with some lift from add-on products and 3-packs. Using that as the central estimate plus a range for AOV variance:

The honest read

The PANDAS market doesn't have a size problem. The bottleneck is reaching the audience efficiently via Meta and giving the creative the volume needed to compound — both of which are work the brand can do. The TAM math works at the conservative end and is comfortable at the central end.

Where this goes next — playing this story for everything it's worth

The reason this funnel is worth investing more time and energy into isn't the offer or the LP architecture — it's the story underneath it. A real adult, who really had PANDAS as a teenager, whose family really spent $50,000 trying to fix his motor tics, who really found the thing that worked. That kind of source material is the hardest thing in DTC to come by. Most brands have to manufacture it. This brand has the real artifact.

The mistake would be treating the current funnel as the final form of how that story gets used. The current funnel is one expression of it. There's a much bigger surface area available — and going deeper on it is where the compounding happens. Two parallel directions to play:

Direction 1 — More creative assets supporting the current funnel

The funnel works. What it needs is volume of creative pointing at it. The quiver of arrows idea from the synthesis above: 20 distinct ads beats 1 ad with 20× the budget. Each ad is an angle that catches a different person on a different day in a different mood. The funnel doesn't change. The doors into it multiply.

The headline on the image is a single, consistent anchor; the lead-in changes around it.

Direction 2 — Sibling funnels for the same avatar, different angles on the same story

The current funnel hinges on a specific lead-in: the financial weight of $50,000 in failed treatments. That's one valid door into the survivor's story — but it's not the only one. The same arc can be expressed through different headlines, each pulling a different lever out of the same true source material. None of these require a new story. They require a new door into the existing one.

A few examples of what that looks like, all rooted in the same survivor and the same arc:

  • The "tried everything" lead-in. "I tried fish oil. I tried magnesium. I tried two SSRIs and a behavioral therapist. None of them stopped my motor tics. This finally did." Same survivor. Same PANDAS arc. Different door. Catches the parent who's been working a checklist of natural-first solutions for their child's tics and is running out of items.
  • The "wrong diagnosis" lead-in. "For two years, four doctors told me my motor tics were Tourette's. They were wrong. It was PANDAS — and once we treated it as PANDAS, the tics finally stopped." Catches the parent whose child has been diagnosed with Tourette's and is starting to suspect the label doesn't fit.
  • The "40 medications" lead-in. "By the time I was seventeen I'd been on more than forty medications for my motor tics. None of them worked. One thing finally did — and no neurologist had ever mentioned it." A real detail from the LP — catches the pharma-skeptical parent watching their child cycle through prescriptions for tics that don't slow down.
  • The "negative strep test" lead-in. "My strep swab came back negative. It still took us fourteen more months to figure out PANDAS was driving my motor tics the whole time." Catches the parent who's been told a negative test rules out PANDAS — when in fact most pediatricians miss it.
  • The "single moment" lead-in. "I remember the Tuesday afternoon my shoulder first jerked. I was fourteen. Nobody told me for three years that what I had was PANDAS, and that motor tics like mine had a name and a fix." Catches the parent in the first weeks of sudden-onset tics, looking for someone who's been through exactly what they're watching.

Each lead-in implies its own advertorial, its own image set, its own ad concepts — but all of them lead the reader to the same destination: the survivor's confession, the failed-treatment arc, and the one thing that worked. The buyer doesn't change. The arc doesn't change. The door changes.

This is the opposite of building more funnels for more avatars. It's building more funnels for the same avatar, each catching them in a different state of awareness. Most brands can't do this because their underlying story isn't true or rare enough to support multiple angles without contradicting itself. This brand's story has that capacity, and most of it is unused.

The thing worth not overlooking

The temptation, when a funnel is doing 1.36x ROAS at modest spend, is to read it as a small result and move on to another avatar. The opposite read is the right one: this is the only cold-acquisition funnel in the entire account above first-order break-even, and the source material under it — a real survivor, a real $50K spend, a real recovery — is the hardest asset in DTC to replicate. The downside of going deep here is bounded. The upside is a category that few competitors can ever compete with on the same terms.

The recommendation isn't to abandon the other funnels. It's to recognize that the magic is in this story, this avatar, this niche — and to stop treating one funnel as the full expression of it. There is room here for ten advertorials, dozens of ad concepts, and a year of compounding work, all hinging on a story the brand already has and most competitors will never get.
