6 · Recommendation: pause this funnel

The funnel as it currently runs should be paused. Not killed permanently — paused. Here's the case for why, in plain terms:

Why pause

  • It's losing −$12.48 per customer over 60 days. That's not a "wait and see" problem — every dollar of FG ad spend is currently producing a sub-$0.40 dollar of revenue back. The economics aren't there.
  • The repeat behavior signals freebie-seekers, not buyers. 8.8% came back to claim ANOTHER free gummy. 1.6% bought a paid product, and most of those were also discounted to near-free at checkout. Only one of the 9 "paid repeaters" paid full list price for anything in 60 days.
  • Zero subscriptions started. The thesis "free gummy → eventual subscriber" isn't showing up in 60 days of data. If it were a quiet conversion engine, we'd see at least a few subscription starts among 547 customers.
  • None of the 547 bought a higher-margin SKU. No kids drops. No 750mg or 1000mg. No CBN drops. The customer base produced by this funnel is not the customer base that buys profitable products.
  • Continuing the funnel costs ~$2,850/month in CM. That budget could fund ~$2,800 of testing on a different acquisition path that may have a real chance of being profitable.

What "pause" looks like

  • Turn off all Meta campaigns pointed at /pages/free-gummies and /products/free-cbd-gummies.
  • Leave the LP itself live (won't affect organic).
  • Redirect the freed budget to test a single alternative acquisition angle on a measurable bar: must produce CM-positive customers within 30 days.

When to reconsider

The funnel could be revisited if any of these change:

  • The free-gummy LP gets a one-time-offer (OTO) added at the cart step that converts at meaningful rate. The current funnel asks for nothing beyond the free claim — it has no upsell mechanism. Without that, the unit economics will not change.
  • Fulfillment cost drops materially (a different 3PL, smaller-pack shipping). At $7.40/unit fulfillment, the offer can't be CM-neutral even at $9.95 shipping.
  • The offer changes structure — e.g., "free + ship + paid product attached" — turning the funnel into a loss-leader bundle rather than a single-SKU freebie.
The bar: a free-product acquisition funnel for a bootstrapped brand needs to either (a) be CM-neutral on the first order through an OTO/upsell, or (b) recoup its CAC inside the first 30 days through a clear second-purchase path. This funnel does neither. Until one of those structural changes happens, scaling it deeper grows the loss linearly.

8 · Why "first-order break-even" is the bar — not a preference

There are two completely different versions of this funnel question, and they have completely different right answers.

Version A — VC-funded: Spend $5,000 today. Lose $12 per customer. Get back the loss (and more) at month 6 through subscriptions, retention, brand effect, lifetime value. Burn now, harvest later. This is workable when you have 18 months of runway in the bank and the only thing standing between you and a Series B is showing growth.

Version B — Bootstrapped: Spend $5,000 today. Need that $5,000 back fast enough to fund another $5,000 of acquisition next month. Cash in → cash out → cash back in. The whole machine works on the speed of that cycle.

GVN is running Version B. So the question isn't "will this funnel net out positive at some unknown month in the future?" — the question is "does this funnel return its acquisition cost fast enough to fund the next batch of acquisitions?"

Why the timescale actually matters

Imagine GVN spends $10,000/month on the free gummy funnel. At the current rate, that's ~880 customers acquired and a CM loss of ~$11,000 per month. Over six months, that's $66,000 of cash gone — paid for in advance, not recouped on rolling basis.

Even if every one of those 5,280 customers eventually converted and produced positive LTV at month 6, two things would still be true:

  1. The cash to fund acquisition months 2-6 has to come from somewhere else. Either profits from other funnels, or savings, or debt. A bootstrapped business doesn't have a Series A to absorb six months of losses.
  2. The compounding works in reverse. Each month the funnel runs at a loss, less cash is available to acquire customers next month — including through funnels that do break even. The unprofitable funnel quietly drains the budget that profitable funnels need to scale.

The math of bootstrapped growth depends on fast cash recycling. A funnel that's CM-positive on the first order (or at minimum CM-neutral within 30 days) means each $1 of ad spend generates $1+ of cash back, which can fund the next $1 of ad spend. That's how a bootstrapped business goes from $1,000/day in ad spend to $5,000/day — not by raising money, but by scaling on its own returns.

A funnel that needs 90+ days to break even can only scale at the speed of whatever else is producing cash. If the rest of the business is producing $20K/month of free cash, the funnel can scale to a level where its 90-day-deferred losses are absorbed by that $20K. Anything beyond that, and the business runs out of cash before the funnel ever delivers.

What this means for the free gummy specifically

The free gummy funnel as it currently runs is structurally Version A — running inside a Version B business. It only works if there's a long-tail recovery phase that we cannot currently see, and even if that phase exists, GVN doesn't have the cash buffer to wait 90+ days for it.

The economics aren't close. At −$12.48 per customer, the funnel isn't a small tweak away from working — it's a structural mismatch. Pausing isn't a verdict on whether the offer could work in some other version of GVN with deeper pockets and a longer time horizon. It's a recognition that the funnel's economics don't fit the cash cycle of a bootstrapped business at this stage.

The general principle worth keeping

For any acquisition funnel a bootstrapped DTC brand runs, the test isn't "will this be profitable eventually?" It's:

Does each dollar of ad spend produce ≥ $1 of cash back fast enough to fund the next dollar of ad spend?

If yes — scale it. If no, and the gap is small enough that obvious changes could close it — fix it. If the gap is wide and there's no clear path to closing it within the cash cycle the business operates on — pause it.

The cost of running an unprofitable funnel isn't just the dollars it loses; it's every customer that didn't get acquired through a profitable funnel because the cash was already spent.
